Introduction

1 question
What are the critiques of land acknowledgements, from the left and from the right? +

From the left Land acknowledgements are seen as performative and hollow — they allow settlers to feel moral without making any real structural change. They do not return land or redistribute power, and can actually legitimize the current colonial order by making it seem like something is being done. The acknowledgement becomes a ritual that replaces action rather than prompting it.

From the right Land acknowledgements are seen as divisive and politically motivated — imposing collective guilt on people who had no personal role in colonization. Critics argue they undermine national unity and are virtue-signalling imposed on public life.

Democracy

6 questions
What are the four key institutions of democratic accountability and why is each important? +
  • Elections: Create accountability — politicians must keep promises or lose re-election. They set up a bridge between voters and power-holders.
  • Independent judiciary: Upholds the rule of law and checks the executive. Threatening courts is dangerous because it weakens checks and balances and undermines the principle that everyone is equal under the law.
  • Free press: Exposes government wrongdoing, shapes public opinion, and makes the costs of war visible (e.g. Vietnam). Holds power to account.
  • Civil society / NGOs: Exert continuous pressure on elected officials, expose wrongdoing, force new issues onto the political agenda.
Why isn't the constitution sufficient to safeguard democracy? +

Constitutions are inherently incomplete — they cannot specify behavior for every situation and leave room for interpretation and exploitation. Many countries with U.S.-style constitutions have still experienced democratic breakdown.

Political actors can follow the letter of the law while violating its spirit. Like a "work-to-rule" strike — workers obey every written rule but refuse all informal cooperation, causing the system to collapse — democracy cannot function on written rules alone without informal restraint. Democratic stability depends on unwritten norms that regulate behavior where formal law is silent or ambiguous.

What norms operate as the soft guardrails of democracy? +

Two fundamental unwritten norms: mutual toleration and institutional forbearance.

These informal guardrails prevent ordinary political competition from turning into destructive conflict. When they erode, democracies can collapse even while constitutional rules remain formally intact. Note: democracy can die legally, gradually, and even with popular support when these norms disappear.

Define mutual toleration and institutional forbearance and explain why they are important. +

Mutual toleration Political rivals accept one another as legitimate. Competitors recognize opponents have an equal right to run, win elections, and govern. Losing is not an existential disaster; winning does not justify eliminating the opposition.

When it breaks down, opponents are framed as traitors or existential threats, and extraordinary measures (repression, power grabs) seem justified. Example: Spain in the 1930s — polarization led to democratic collapse.

Institutional forbearance Exercising restraint in the use of legal and constitutional powers. Not using every available tool to its maximum extent simply because it is legal to do so. Aggressively exploiting loopholes or rarely-used powers ("constitutional hardball") destabilizes the system and provokes retaliation.

Why they matter together: When toleration declines, politics becomes a fight for survival. When forbearance disappears, institutions become weapons. Together, their erosion produces a downward spiral — democratic competition becomes permanent crisis. Key case: Chile 1970s under Allende.

What are the "institutions of democracy?" +

The formal institutions include: elections, an independent judiciary, a free press, checks and balances (separation of powers, federalism), and civil society / NGOs. Together they create accountability and prevent concentration of power. However, institutions alone are insufficient without the informal norms of mutual toleration and forbearance.

What were the differences among the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd waves of democracy and what caused each? +
  • 1st wave (19th c. – 1920s): Emerged from Enlightenment ideas and rejection of monarchy. Limited — voting restricted to male property owners. Caused by the rise of liberalism, parliamentary supremacy (e.g. Glorious Revolution 1688), and industrialization.
  • 2nd wave (post-WWII): Allied victory over fascism and decolonization spread democratic governance to new states. Short-lived in many cases — many slid back into authoritarianism.
  • 3rd wave (1970s onward): Democracy became nearly universal. Driven by collapse of authoritarian regimes in Southern Europe, Latin America, and especially the USSR collapse. However, since ~2000 many electoral democracies have slid back into electoral autocracies — this is the current democratic backsliding crisis.

Political participation

4 questions
Why do people participate in politics? +

Schlozman, Verba & Brady identify four types of rewards that motivate participation:

  • Selective material benefits: Jobs, career advancement — rare, not the main driver.
  • Selective social gratifications: Enjoyment, social interaction, recognition — important in group activities (campaigning, protesting).
  • Selective civic gratifications: The most commonly cited reason. Civic duty, doing one's share, improving the community. Many cite only civic motivations.
  • Collective policy outcomes: Desire to influence government — 61% of voters say they vote hoping to impact policy.

Participation is expressive, norm-based, and identity-driven — not merely instrumentally rational.

What is a public good? A free rider? +

Public good A good that is non-excludable (you cannot stop people from benefiting) and non-rivalrous (one person's benefit doesn't reduce another's). Political participation produces public goods — everyone benefits from good governance whether or not they participated.

Free rider Someone who benefits from a public good without contributing to it. Rational choice theory predicts that because everyone benefits regardless, the rational move is to do nothing and let others do the work — leading to predicted mass apathy.

How does this article prove that rational choice theorists are wrong about political participation? +

Rational choice theory predicts nonparticipation: individual action has negligible impact, and public goods create free-rider incentives. But the Citizen Participation Study (15,000 survey respondents; 2,517 follow-up interviews) found widespread participation across all forms of political activity.

The reason: people participate because it is intrinsically rewarding — through civic duty, identity, social enjoyment, and expressive satisfaction. Rational choice theory is too narrow when limited to material self-interest. Expanding it to include civic rewards risks tautology, but the data shows participation is not paradoxical — it is meaningful.

What is a limitation of institutionalized participation? +

Institutionalized participation (voting, campaigns, working within the system) systematically privileges the wealthy, educated, and organized. Not all groups can participate equally — political influence is stratified by income and race. As Woodly puts it: "the heavenly chorus sings with an upper-class accent."

Institutions also tend toward bureaucratization and elite capture over time. Interest group pluralism reduces justice claims to competitive bargaining, produces cynicism rather than citizenship, and depoliticizes public life — citizens forget how to act politically together.

Social movements

4 questions
How do you measure the success of a social movement? +
  • Gathering large-scale public support for the cause
  • Changing government policy directly
  • Changing public opinion on an issue
  • Becoming institutionalized as an organization with lasting political presence

Success is not only policy wins — movements also succeed by changing meanings, ideas, identities, and expectations of democracy.

How do different social movement theories explain the success or failure of a movement? +

Resource mobilization theory Movements succeed to the extent they can access and mobilize resources: money, logistics, social media, symbolic capital, allies. Example: The Freedom Convoy had trucks, money, social media reach, former military for logistics, and allies in law enforcement.

Political opportunity model Movements arise when there is a weakness or opening in the political system. Example: The Civil Rights Movement exploited the ideological gap between the federal government and southern states that were violating constitutional rights — D.C. turned a blind eye, creating a political opportunity.

Framing processes The language and symbols movements use to legitimate their presence and demands. Effective framing builds public legitimacy. Examples: "I can't breathe," "We are a sovereign nation," "Canada is a free country."

What is Woodly's overall argument in the chapter you read? +

Woodly argues that social movements are not disruptions of democracy or merely pressure tactics to gain access to policymakers — they are essential democratic institutions, a "Fifth Estate" alongside the legislature, executive, judiciary, and press.

Democratic institutions tend toward oligarchic capture and bureaucratic stagnation. When they become unresponsive, a "politics of despair" sets in — collective belief that political action cannot produce change, leading to withdrawal from citizenship. Social movements counter this: they re-politicize public life, revive citizenship, restore democratic legitimacy, and serve as democracy's method of self-correction. Democracy cannot endure without them.

What can non-institutionalized participation do that institutionalized participation cannot? +

Non-institutionalized participation (protests, strikes, marches, boycotts, blockades) can:

  • Force issues onto the agenda that institutionalized channels ignore or suppress
  • Disrupt the normal schedule to make power-holders respond — it is aimed at being seen
  • Transform political possibilities and reconstruct democratic meaning beyond incremental policy
  • Re-politicize citizens who feel shut out of formal institutions
  • Challenge oligarchic capture that elections alone cannot address
  • Restore belief in political agency — remind citizens they are authors of their collective fate

Liberalism

7 questions
Ryan states that liberals' political concerns have altered over the past three centuries. How have they altered, and what has that led to? +

Classical liberalism (17th–18th c.): Emerged from the struggle against monarchy (Glorious Revolution 1688 established parliamentary supremacy). Focused on negative freedom — freedom from government interference. Core concerns: personal liberty, limited government, equality before the law, consent of the governed.

19th c. reform / modern liberalism: Recognized that formal freedom is meaningless without the capacity to exercise it. Led to positive freedom — government must actively enable freedom through welfare, education, redistribution. This produced the welfare state.

This shift created the central tension in liberalism: classical liberals prioritize private property and liberty over equality; modern liberals accept redistribution to enable meaningful freedom. These two strands often conflict but both belong to the liberal tradition.

What's the communitarian critique of liberalism? +

Communitarians argue that liberalism's focus on the individual ignores the importance of community, shared values, and social bonds in forming identity and moral life. Liberalism treats individuals as atomistic and self-sufficient — detached from the communities, traditions, and relationships that give their lives meaning. This undermines the social fabric. Sandel's critique of meritocracy is related: even if it worked, it destroys solidarity and tells those who fail that they deserve to fail.

What's the difference between classical and modern liberalism? +

Classical liberalism Freedom = absence of government interference (negative freedom). Minimal government — only protects against external enemies and internal harm. Values private property above economic equality. Liberty is a birthright.

Modern/social liberalism Freedom = capacity to actually exercise choices (positive freedom). Government must enable freedom through welfare and social support. Everyone should be wealthy enough to exercise free choice. Accepts some redistribution to make freedom meaningful.

Classical liberals believe in minimal government. What does that mean? +

The government's role is strictly limited to:

  • Protecting citizens from external enemies (national defense)
  • Protecting individuals from harm by others within society

The state should not redistribute wealth, impose a moral vision, or regulate beyond basic protection. Rights protect the individual from other individuals and from the state itself. There is a tradeoff: generating equality through redistribution violates private property — classical liberals choose freedom over equality.

What's the difference between positive and negative freedom? +

Negative freedom Freedom from interference — the absence of external constraints imposed by the state or others. It occurs naturally without government action. Examples: free speech, freedom of religion, right to private property, right of political opposition.

Positive freedom Freedom as capacity — having the real ability and resources to make meaningful choices. Requires government to actively provide conditions (education, healthcare, income support). Without this, formal freedoms are hollow for those without means.

Is there a liberal theory of society? What is it? +

Yes. Liberalism holds that society is composed of free, rational individuals whose rights pre-exist the state. Society is organized around protecting those individual rights. Key elements:

  • The state must be secular — religion is a personal matter, not a basis for public law (religious wars were destructive)
  • The law applies equally to all
  • Pluralism — different people pursuing different visions of the good life is accepted and valued
  • No single communal moral vision is imposed on all

Democracy is about extending power to the people; liberalism is about setting limits on that power.

How is a constitution a conservative political instrument? +

A constitution is conservative because it limits what majorities can vote on and change — it entrenches past decisions and makes them very difficult to alter. This protects individual rights from democratic majorities.

Example: Even if most Canadians wanted to require uncovered faces for citizenship oaths, the constitution (Charter of Rights) overrode this majority preference to protect religious freedom. By constraining democratic will, the constitution preserves the liberal order. It is conservative in the sense that it conserves existing rights arrangements against popular pressure.

The politics of individual responsibility

4 questions
How do Wiedeman and Wise link access to credit to race? +

Wiedeman and Wise argue that access to credit has historically been racialized — Black Americans and minorities were systematically denied mortgages and loans through practices like redlining and discriminatory lending. This prevented wealth accumulation through homeownership and business investment, entrenching racial wealth gaps.

When credit was later expanded to minorities, it was often through predatory lending, exposing them to disproportionate risk. The 2008 subprime mortgage crisis devastated racialized communities hardest. The credit system therefore appears neutral but produces and sustains racial inequality.

What is the connection between conservative political stances and easy access to credit? +

Easy credit can substitute for government social programs — it allows people to consume and maintain living standards through private debt rather than state redistribution. This aligns with conservative politics: rather than taxing and redistributing, credit lets individuals "solve" their economic problems privately.

This makes structural inequality less visible and reduces political pressure for government intervention. Credit performs a politically conservative function by individualizing economic problems that are structurally caused.

Sandel says "The liberal left's pursuit of meritocracy has betrayed the working classes." How? +

Sandel argues the liberal left embraced meritocracy as the solution to inequality — if everyone gets a fair shot through education and equal opportunity, success becomes deserved. But this betrays working people in two ways:

  • It blames those who don't rise for their own failure ("you have no one to blame but yourself"), stripping them of dignity
  • It made the left condescending and credential-obsessed, focused on university degrees and elite expertise, abandoning the moral and cultural concerns of working-class communities who feel looked down upon
How does Sandel critique the left-liberal consensus on merit? +

Even if meritocracy worked perfectly, Sandel argues it is morally problematic. It assumes success is fully the result of individual talent and effort, ignoring luck, inherited advantage, and structural conditions.

The "tyranny of merit": those who succeed are told they deserve it; those who fail are told the same. This humiliates those who don't advance and erodes solidarity across class lines. The left-liberal consensus celebrated getting ahead through credentials, but this produced a politics that alienated working people and hollowed out shared community values.

Critical race theory

4 questions
Define critical race theory. +

CRT holds that race is a social construction — it has no biological basis (genetic differences within races are larger than between races), but humans have imbued it with meaning, creating hierarchies and boundaries. "We create the world by imbuing some things with meaning."

CRT argues that racism is not merely individual prejudice but is embedded in laws, policies, institutions, and social structures. It challenges the liberal assumption that formal legal equality is enough to address racial inequality — ostensibly neutral systems perpetuate racial disadvantage.

How do liberals deal with racial discrimination? +

Liberals use colourblindness — the law should treat everyone equally regardless of race. Remove explicit racial barriers, ensure formal equality before the law. Any remaining inequality is treated as an individual or cultural shortcoming. Liberalism's formal stance of equality makes the system appear fair and neutral, treating racism as individual aberration rather than systemic structure.

What is colourblindness and how does it perpetuate racial discrimination? +

Colourblindness: The legal principle that race should not be taken into account — treat everyone equally regardless of race.

How it perpetuates discrimination:

  • By refusing to acknowledge race, it obscures how laws and policies continue to disadvantage people of colour
  • Assumes existing inequalities reflect individual or cultural shortcomings rather than systemic discrimination
  • Its ostensible neutrality sustains inequalities that already exist — it maintains the racial status quo
  • Allows those in power to avoid confronting racial disparities — "There may be a few individual racists, but the system itself is neutral"
Give 2 examples of ostensibly neutral institutions or policies that are actually racist. +
  • Credit and mortgage lending systems: Presented as neutral market mechanisms, but redlining and discriminatory lending systematically excluded Black Americans from wealth-building through homeownership — entrenching the racial wealth gap under the guise of financial neutrality.
  • Drug laws / criminal justice: Drug laws apply equally on paper, but enforcement, prosecution, and sentencing have disproportionately targeted Black and Indigenous communities — producing racialized incarceration outcomes without any explicit racial language in the law.

Citizenship and immigration

5 questions
What is the difference between ethnic and civic citizenship? Provide examples. +

Ethnic citizenship Based on descent and ancestry — you are a citizen because of who your parents are, not where you were born or what you pledge. Citizenship = belonging to a particular ethnic group. Example: Germany historically (jus sanguinis — citizenship through German blood).

Civic citizenship Based on allegiance to the state and its laws — you pledge to uphold its values and rules. Ethnicity, religion, or origin does not determine membership. Examples: Canada and the United States (birthright citizenship plus naturalization based on residency and oath).

What is a liberal citizenship regime? +

A liberal citizenship regime is organized around civic (not ethnic) principles. It is open to immigrants and newcomers regardless of ethnicity or religion, uses multiculturalism as its integration strategy (respecting cultural differences within the nation), and grants rights on the basis of residency and civic participation. It resists exclusion or forced assimilation — both of which are anti-liberal strategies.

How do countries integrate and fail to integrate immigrants? +

Successful: Multiculturalism — recognizing and respecting cultural differences within the nation while offering full civic participation and rights. The liberal approach.

Failed strategies:

  • Exclusion — denying immigrants full membership and rights; produces marginalization and resentment. Anti-liberal.
  • Forced assimilation — requiring immigrants to abandon their culture and identity to belong. Also anti-liberal; produces conflict and identity loss.
What does it mean to relocate the source of citizenship rights from the state to personhood? +

Traditionally, rights are granted by the state to its citizens. To relocate rights to personhood means grounding rights in the fact of being human — not in state membership. This means even non-citizens (migrants, refugees, undocumented people) hold basic rights by virtue of their humanity. It challenges state sovereignty over who receives rights and has major implications for how we treat immigrants and asylum seekers internationally.

How will immigration change in the next thirty years? +

Immigration will increase significantly, driven primarily by climate change — rising sea levels, extreme weather, drought, and crop failures will displace large populations, creating climate refugees. Most migration currently happens within countries (and is therefore invisible), but cross-border migration will intensify enormously. This will put massive pressure on immigration systems, citizenship regimes, and liberal values around the world.

War

3 questions
What is an independent variable? Dependent variable? +

Dependent variable The outcome you are trying to explain — what is caused. Example: Russia's invasion of Ukraine.

Independent variable The cause or explanation — the factor that drives the outcome. Example: Putin's desire to rebuild the Russian empire.

The dependent variable depends on the independent variable.

Explain the three levels of analysis and identify examples. (Including the democratic peace theory example.) +

Individual level War is explained by the characteristics of individual leaders — their psychology, beliefs, misperceptions, cognitive biases. Example: Putin's imperial ambitions explain Russia's invasion of Ukraine.

State / national level War is explained by the internal characteristics of states — regime type, nature of society, economic development. Democratic Peace Theory fits here: democracies don't fight each other, because of democratic norms, institutional constraints, and public accountability (citizens don't vote to send themselves to war; free press exposes horror; political oppositions undermine military resolve). It fits the state level because it depends on the nature of the regime.

International system level War is explained by the structure of the global system — anarchy (no world government), balance of power, power transitions. Realist theory: states act rationally to advance security in an anarchic system. Power Transition Theory: war is more likely when a rising power challenges the dominant state (hegemon). The primary determinant is the distribution of power at the systemic level.

What is just war theory and what are the elements of a just war? +

Just war theory provides moral criteria for evaluating whether a war is justified. Three components:

Jus ad Bellum — right to go to war:

  • Just cause
  • Right intention
  • Legitimate authority
  • Last resort
  • Proportionality
  • Reasonable chance of success

Jus in Bello — justice in the conduct of war:

  • Discrimination (distinguishing combatants from civilians)
  • Proportionality
  • No evil means
  • Treatment of prisoners

Jus post Bellum — justice after war:

  • Restoration of peace
  • Reconstruction of justice
  • Proportionality and restraint

Climate change

6 questions
What's the overall argument Green makes in her article? +

Green argues that framing climate change as a matter of individual responsibility (personal carbon footprints, consumer choices, recycling) is politically harmful. This framing — promoted in part by fossil fuel interests — deflects attention from structural and systemic causes, individualizes a collective political problem, and makes large-scale political solutions harder to achieve. It makes people feel personally guilty rather than politically mobilized for systemic change.

How does the "existential politics" framework propose solving the climate change problem? +

The existential politics framework treats climate change as an urgent, civilization-level threat requiring transformative political action — not incremental policy adjustments. It calls for treating climate as an emergency requiring mobilization at the scale of wartime: restructuring economies, replacing energy systems, and building political coalitions powerful enough to overcome the fossil fuel industry. The urgency and scale require going beyond normal interest-group bargaining politics.

What is the negative impact of ISDS on climate policy? +

ISDS (Investor-State Dispute Settlement) is a mechanism in trade agreements that allows corporations to sue governments for policies that reduce the value of their investments. This creates a "regulatory chill" — governments fear enacting strong climate policies (like phasing out fossil fuels) because they could be sued by energy companies for lost profits. ISDS protects corporate investment interests at the direct expense of climate action, locking governments into the fossil fuel economy.

Why is it better to think of climate change as a global fractal problem instead of a collective action problem? +

The collective action framing treats climate as one giant global problem where all countries must cooperate simultaneously — making it seem intractable because any country can free-ride and defect.

The fractal framing (Bernstein & Hoffman) recognizes that the problem exists at multiple levels simultaneously — local, national, and global — each with its own politics and power dynamics. This opens up the possibility of action at multiple scales without requiring universal global agreement first, and reveals how local and national decisions are structurally part of the same problem as the global one.

What is a fractal carbon trap? +

A fractal carbon trap refers to the way carbon-intensive systems are self-reinforcing at every level of analysis. Political, economic, and social structures at local, national, and global scales all lock in fossil fuel dependence — infrastructure, investment, trade agreements, and political coalitions all make transition away from carbon very difficult, regardless of which level you examine. The trap reproduces itself across scales like a fractal pattern.

How can framing climate change as a matter of individual responsibility be problematic? (The example linking climate change and personal responsibility.) +

The example from Bernstein & Hoffman: Framing infant health as a matter of individual breastfeeding choices makes the issue personal and moralistic — but it obscures the structural causes: the absence of maternity leave in the US, the scarcity of doctors and healthcare in Canada, inadequate public support for new parents.

Similarly, framing climate change around personal carbon footprints makes structural injustices invisible, individualizes a collective political problem, and reduces pressure on governments and corporations. This links the climate week to the personal responsibility week: the politics of individual responsibility can serve to depoliticize and conceal structural problems — whether in health, economics, or the environment.

Reconciliation

2 questions
What are Canada's goals in the politics of reconciliation? +

Canada's goals are primarily to acknowledge past wrongs (especially the residential school system), rebuild trust with Indigenous peoples, and achieve national unity. Key mechanisms: the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (2009–2015), financial compensation through the Indian Residential Schools Settlement Agreement, and formal apologies.

Critics argue Canada used reconciliation as a political opportunity to clean its moral history — the TRC's opening framing was an urgent desire to put the bad past behind and move forward. Canada also fulfills legal obligations (the "duty to consult" in the Constitution), though Indigenous peoples argue this falls short of genuine consent.

What are the goals of many Indigenous people in the politics of reconciliation? +

Many Indigenous peoples seek not just acknowledgment but real structural transformation:

  • Return of land and resources
  • Genuine self-determination and self-governance
  • Decolonization — not just integration into the existing system
  • Replacement of the "duty to consult" with free, prior, and informed consent (as in UNDRIP)
  • Addressing ongoing injustices — not just historical ones (the residential school legacy persists in child welfare, health, incarceration, poverty)
  • Revitalization of language, culture, and community

Indigenous people found the apology irrational because the residential school system's effects had not ended — its legacy still persisted. Reconciliation as practiced by Canada is therefore seen as insufficient and often as a way for settlers to relieve guilt without real change.

Political hope

3 questions
What is the overall point of the lecture on political hope? +

Political hope is not naive optimism — it is a necessary resource for democratic participation. In the face of a "politics of despair" (declining trust, rising inequality, political alienation), hope is what motivates people to remain engaged rather than withdraw. Without hope, citizens disengage, and democracy deteriorates not through a coup but through quiet withdrawal. Political hope is therefore a democratic necessity, not just a personal feeling.

Describe at least three examples of reasons for political hope. +
  • Social movements have produced real change: The Civil Rights Movement, women's suffrage, LGBTQ+ rights movements, and the Movement for Black Lives all achieved what seemed impossible — demonstrating that collective action works and structural change is possible.
  • Democratic backsliding has been reversed: Countries that slid toward autocracy have recovered through citizen resistance and institutional resilience, showing that democratic erosion is not inevitable.
  • Youth political engagement: Young people (the most likely to protest, even if least likely to vote) are driving movements like climate strikes and March for Our Lives — demonstrating the next generation's political vitality and capacity to reshape the agenda.
  • Global progress on major problems: Extreme poverty has declined, child mortality has fallen, and access to education has expanded — evidence that collective political will can solve enormous problems over time.
What is the value of political hope for democracy? +

Political hope sustains democratic participation. When citizens believe political action cannot produce change — the "politics of despair" — they withdraw from citizenship, become cynical, and accept elite dominance. This produces declining voter turnout, depoliticization, and ultimately the collapse of the public sphere.

Hope counters this by maintaining belief in political agency — the sense that citizens are the authors of their collective fate. Social movements are especially important here: they restore hope by demonstrating that change is possible, that the political imagination can expand, and that ordinary people acting together have power. Without hope, democracy collapses quietly from within.
